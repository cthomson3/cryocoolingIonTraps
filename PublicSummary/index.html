Why Do We Care About Trapping Ions?

Many areas of modern physics require extremely precise measurements; To make these measurements, scientists often need to hold tiny charged particles still.


However, atoms and ions naturally move around very quickly. The more they move around, the harder it is to make precise measurements of their properties. By trapping them and reducing their motion, we can measure their properties far more precisely.


Ion traps are used in research areas such as: nuclear physics, precision measurements, and in the development of quantum computing.

What is an Ion Trap?

Ion traps help researchers understand how the centres of atoms, called nuclei, behave. They also help us learn how different elements are created inside stars through nuclear reactions. An ion trap is a device that controls the movement of charged particles, using electricity.


Ions are atoms which have gained or lost one or more electrons; giving them an electric charge. As ions are charged, they can be controlled using electric fields. An ion trap uses carefully designed electric fields to hold these particles in a small region in space, like an invisible cage made of electric forces.


By keeping the ions trapped and nearly motionless, physicists can study their behaviour with extreme detail; allowing precise measurements of their properties to be taken. Below you can see images of a classic ion trap and the ion trap used in this project.

What is a PCB RFQ?

A PCB RFQ was the main type of ion trap used in the project.


PCB stands for printed circuit board. This is the same technology used in most everyday electronic devices, from smartphones to washing machines, allowing us to control the ions trapped inside easily. Using PCBs allows the trap to be manufactured in a cheap, compact, and precise manner.


RFQ stands for radio-frequency quadrupole. RF refers to electrical signals that change direction very rapidly inside the trap. Quadrupole refers to the arrangement of the electrodes inside the trap, creating the electric field, which is designed to push ions back into the centre of the trap. These rapidly changing, highly structured electric fields create a stable trapping region in the centre of the device, confining ions.

How Does Cooling to Extremely Low Temperatures Affect Ions?

At high temperatures, particles move quickly and erratically. As we lower the temperature, their motion becomes slower. Even when particles are trapped in an ion trap, they still vibrate and move slightly.


Cooling the trap reduces this motion. The slower the particles move, the easier they are to control and measure precisely.

How to Reach Cryogenic Temperatures?

Now that we seen why cryogenic ion traps are needed, you may be wondering: "How can we cool our trap to temperatures that low?" The following video, by Sunpower Inc, shows an animation explaining how our cryocooler works (this link will open in a new tab):

How does a Sunpower Cryocooler Work? - YouTube


In ideal conditions, the Sunpower cryocooler can achieve temperatures as low as 40 Kelvin, about -230°C, which is roughly 12 times as cold as a typical household freezer. So in the same way that a freezer left open cannot cool its contents, the cryocooler must be kept in a vacuum with limited contact points to prevent heat from entering the system.

What Are the Challenges and What Comes Next?

Cooling the ion trap to cryogenic temperatures has proven to be more difficult than expected. Although the cryocooler itself is capable of reaching very low temperatures, cooling the trap has been challenging; any small path for heat to enter the system can significantly reduce our ability to cool the trap.


One key issue is how efficiently heat can move through different materials. The PCB that forms the ion trap does not transfer heat quickly, so it cools slowly. In contrast, the aluminium support structures conduct heat much more effectively, therefore bringing unwanted heat into the system from the outside environment. Improving how heat flows through the system, for example by redesigning the thermal contact points, could allow us to reach lower temperatures more reliably.


Although reaching stable cryogenic temperatures remains a challenge, understanding these limitations has been an important outcome of the project. By improving the cooling design, these PCB-based ion traps could become a compact, cost-effective tool for future high-precision experiments in nuclear physics and beyond.
